\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used as scalable scoring instruments for literary emotion tasks; however, persona prompts and decoding temperature often vary together.
This coupling interprets the stance with stochastic decoding control.
We introduce a fully crossed person-temperature design for Japanese literary emotion scoring and evaluate the score, validity, and normalized fuzzy entropy within the same balanced factorial structure.
The main run used six contemporary models, three literary texts, four personas, four temperature levels, and five repeats per cell, yielding 1440 responses and 5760 emotion-level scores.
After retrying only four initially invalid rows, all records passed through the structured-output gate.
Within each model-text-emotion unit, balanced decomposition summaries show that the mean scores are largely persona-driven and nearly additive, whereas normalized fuzzy entropy carries larger persona-temperature interaction burdens, especially for anger and surprise.
The results show that independent crossing is needed not only for a cleaner descriptive separation of score components but also for detecting when membership-overlap entropy remains jointly sensitive to prompting and sampling controls.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
large language models, persona prompting, temperature, fuzzy entropy, emotion scoring, factorial design

\section{Introduction}
LLM-based emotion scoring offers a practical method for comparing affective interpretations across models and prompt settings.
In prior work on emotional individuality, persona prompting and temperature decoding were used to induce diversity in model responses.
However, when a specific persona is paired with a specific temperature, the resulting score variation cannot be cleanly assigned to either the interpretive stance or decoding stochasticity.
This is a design limitation rather than a statistical limitation: no downstream model can separate two factors that are not independently varied.

This aliasing problem is not specific to literary-emotion scoring.
Many LLM evaluation protocols combine role prompts, response-style instructions, sampling parameters, and repeated generation.
If these controls are changed together, later analyses may attribute an observed score difference to the wrong control.
Therefore, the methodological issue is to separate prompt-side interpretive controls from decoding-side stochastic controls before fitting or summarizing downstream outcomes.

This study addresses this limitation with a fully crossed persona–temperature design, following the standard logic of factorial designs and interactions \cite{montgomery2019design}.
We held the text set and response schema fixed, expressed persona only as an interpretive stance in the prompt, and passed temperature only as an API configuration parameter.
The central question is whether persona effects, temperature effects, and their interaction can be described separately for score-level emotion ratings and fuzzy entropy derived from these ratings.

Our contribution is both methodological and empirical.
First, we defined a conference-scale factorial protocol for LLM-based literary emotion scoring.
Second, we computed the balanced effect components for persona, temperature, and persona-temperature interaction under the same cell structure for score and entropy.
Third, we updated the fuzzy entropy pipeline to use a smooth sigmoid-composed membership family as the primary specification while retaining the prior piecewise-linear family as a sensitivity baseline.
The intended claim is not that one membership function is uniquely correct but that the factorial comparison is made under an explicit and reproducible membership specification.

\section{Related Work}
LLM emotion benchmarks have evaluated affective understanding, empathy, and emotion recognition from several perspectives \cite{sabour2024emobench, paech2024eqbench}.
These efforts establish that LLMs can be compared on emotion-related tasks, but they do not resolve how prompt persona and sampling parameters affect continuous score formation.
Our setting is narrower: we focus on repeated literary emotion scoring in a controlled factorial design.

Persona prompting and decoding temperatures are scientifically different controls.
Persona instructions are prompt-side roles or stance manipulations, whereas temperature is a generation-side sampling parameter.
Surveys and empirical studies of LLM personas have shown that assigned personas can systematically affect model behavior \cite{tseng2024personaSurvey,jiang2024personallm}.
Research on stochastic decoding and temperature shows that sampling controls affect output diversity and task performance \cite{holtzman2020curious,renze2024temperature}.
When these controls are paired rather than independently crossed, the resulting variation is design-aliased.

Fuzzy set theory provides a natural language for representing degrees of membership rather than crisp class labels \cite{zadeh1965fuzzy}.
Shannon's entropy form \cite{shannon1948} and non-probabilistic fuzzy entropy \cite{deluca1972nonprobabilistic} motivate entropy-like measures of fuzziness, although membership values are not treated as probability distributions.
Prior EQ-Fuzzy work used Low, Medium, and High membership values over 0--100 emotion intensity scores to summarize score placement in LLM scoring \cite{shirahama2026mdpi}.
Because literary texts are a meaningful domain for sentiment and emotion analysis \cite{kim2019literaryEmotion}, we used Japanese literary scoring as a fixed task panel.
In this study, entropy is interpreted as membership-overlap entropy under an explicit Low/Medium/High membership family, rather than as a universal measure of human emotional ambiguity.

\section{Method}
Let $x_{m,t,p,\tau,r,e}$ denote a 0--100 score produced by model $m$ for text $t$, persona $p$, temperature $\tau$, repeat $r$, and emotion $e$.
The primary factorial cell is $(m,t,p,\tau)$, with four emotion dimensions recorded for each response:
For each model-text-emotion unit, we summarize the $4 \times 4$ persona-temperature mean table.
Let $\bar{y}_{p\tau}$ denote the repeat mean in persona-temperature cell $(p,\tau)$, with marginal means $\bar{y}_{p\cdot}$ and $\bar{y}_{\cdot\tau}$ and grand mean $\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot}$.
The balanced components are as follows:
\begin{equation}
SS_{\mathrm{persona}} =
4\sum_p(\bar{y}_{p\cdot}-\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
SS_{\mathrm{temp}} =
4\sum_\tau(\bar{y}_{\cdot\tau}-\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}} =
\sum_{p,\tau}
(\bar{y}_{p\tau}-\bar{y}_{p\cdot}-\bar{y}_{\cdot\tau}
+\bar{y}_{\cdot\cdot})^2.
\end{equation}
We report the relative interaction burden
\begin{equation}
B =
\frac{SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}}}
{SS_{\mathrm{persona}} + SS_{\mathrm{temp}} +
SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}}},
\end{equation}
and separability shares $1-B$.
Low interaction burden indicates that persona and temperature are practically separable for the outcome under analysis.
The decomposition is descriptive; it quantifies balanced factorial variation in the observed panel, not the causal effects of API temperature across providers.
The same decomposition is applied to the two outcomes under an identical factorial geometry.
For score analysis, $y$ is the repeat mean emotion score in the persona-temperature cell.
For the entropy analysis, $y$ is the repeat-mean normalized membership-overlap entropy $H^*$ in the same cell.
Thus, the differences between the score and entropy decompositions arise from the outcome definition rather than from a change in design.
Repeat-level variation is not treated as an additional sum-of-squares component; instead, repeat stability is summarized by the within-cell bootstrap intervals in Table~\ref{tab:scis-bootstrap-ci}.
Because $B$ is normalized only over the persona, temperature, and interaction components of a fixed $4\times4$ cell-mean table, it should not be interpreted as a global variance share over models, texts, emotions, or future versions of the model.

\subsection{Design Rationale}
The model and text panels were treated as selected fixed panels.
This choice matches the conference-paper goal of testing whether a deconfounded protocol can estimate separability within a concrete contemporary model panel, rather than inferring population-level effects over all possible models or literary texts.
Temperature was treated as a categorical factor because API temperature scales are provider-mediated and need not have identical behavioral meaning across routes.
Five repeats per cell provide a conference-scale stability check, and larger repeat depths are reserved for journal extension.
Finally, the response schema and manifest rows were immutable analysis units.
Failed rows are repaired only by targeted retry of the same manifest rows; therefore, the repair policy does not change valid observations or alter the factorial design.

For fuzzy entropy, each score is mapped to Low, Medium, and High membership values.
The primary family, denoted \texttt{sigmoid\_s\_v1}, preserves the prior semantic breakpoints: Low decreases over 0--30, Medium rises over 10--30, has a plateau over 30--50, and falls over 50--70, and High rises over 50--100.
Unlike the prior piecewise-linear family \cite{shirahama2026mdpi}, each transition interval $[\ell,h]$ is first mapped by a sigmoid with midpoint $(\ell+h)/2$ and gain $2\log 9/(h-\ell)$, and then passed through a smooth S-function.
This sigmoid-composed S-shape follows recent membership function design considerations that prefer smooth transitions while retaining bounded endpoint behavior \cite{umano2025membership}.
We compute raw fuzzy entropy $H_{\mathrm{raw}} = -\sum_k \mu_k \log_2 \mu_k$ with $0\log 0=0$, and report the normalized value $H^* = H_{\mathrm{raw}}/H_{\max}$, where $H_{\max}$ is deterministically estimated for each membership family on a dense score grid.
Therefore, absolute entropy magnitudes are interpreted only within a fixed membership specification, and the legacy family is retained as a sensitivity baseline.

\section{Experiments}
The main experiment used six contemporary LLMs, three Japanese literary texts, four persona stances, four temperature levels ($0.1$, $0.4$, $0.7$, and $0.9$), and five repetitions per condition.
This results in $6 \times 3 \times 4 \times 4 \times 5 = 1440$ response trials.
Each response was required to return four emotion scores and the corresponding reasons in the fixed response schema.
The model panel consisted of GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Grok 4.20, DeepSeek V3.2, and Llama 4 Maverick.
Temperature was never mentioned in the prompt text; the persona was expressed only as an interpretive stance.

Response validity was structurally defined.
A response was counted as valid only when all four emotion scores were parseable numeric values in $[0,100]$ and the required reason fields were present under the fixed response schema.
The initial main run contained four invalid rows, corresponding to an initial failure rate of $4/1440=0.28\%$.
We repaired these by retrying only the failed manifest rows and merging the successful retry records into a separate repaired raw file, while leaving the original raw output unchanged.
No valid rows were rerun or selectively filtered.

\section{Results}

These intervals are conditional on the selected models, texts, personas, and temperatures; they quantify repeat-level stability within the realized panel, not the uncertainty over the population of possible models or literary texts.
For the mean score, the persona accounted for most of the decomposed variation across all four emotions.
The mean persona share was above 90\% for interest, sadness, and surprise, and remained above 80\% for anger.
The temperature shares were comparatively small.
Score-level interaction burden ranges from 3.4\% for sadness to 13.2\% for anger; thus, score variation is largely separable within the crossed subtables.

The entropy results exhibited a different pattern.
Although persona remains the largest average component for $H^*$, the interaction burdens are higher than for the score in every emotion.
They reached 28.3\% for anger and 19.8\% for surprise, compared with 13.2\% and 7.0\% for the corresponding score summaries.
This means that score means and entropy descriptors answer different questions: scores identify the dominant interpretive stance effect, while entropy summarizes where score placement relative to Low, Medium, and High remains jointly sensitive to persona and temperature.

This score-entropy contrast is the main empirical value in the factorial design.
If only the mean scores were reported, the conclusion would be that persona and temperature are mostly separable in the selected panel.
Entropy analysis shows where separability weakens: the same score table can remain nearly additive while the fuzziness of score placement retains a larger interaction component.
A diagonal design that observes only one temperature per persona would not expose this metric-specific difference.

\section{Discussion}
The experiment is best understood as a design diagnostic rather than as a model leaderboard.
The six models were not compared to determine which model was the most emotionally accurate; they formed a contemporary panel for testing whether the prompt stance and decoding temperature could be separated in a structured scoring pipeline.
This framing is important because the same model can appear stable under one metric and interaction-sensitive under another.
The factorial protocol exposes this distinction by forcing every persona to be observed at every temperature.

The key point is not that the crossed design provides a better estimate of a quantity already available from a diagonal design.
This makes a different quantity observable.
When each persona is observed at only one temperature, the persona and temperature are structurally aliased, and the persona-temperature interaction component cannot be identified from the design.
Therefore, the fully crossed table is a prerequisite for asking whether the score and entropy outcomes have the same separability profile.

The main substantive implication is the metric-specific separability.
Mean scores are mostly additive in persona and temperature within the selected model text emotion subtables.
This supports treating persona as a dominant prompt-side component in the present decomposition model.
However, the normalized fuzzy entropy retains a larger interaction burden.
Thus, entropy should not be treated as a redundant transformation of the score mean.
It captures how scores move relative to Low, Medium, and High membership regions, and that placement can remain jointly sensitive to prompt framing and sampling even when score means are nearly separable.

This distinction is methodological rather than leaderboard oriented.
A protocol that reports only mean scores would suggest that persona and temperature are mostly separable in the present study.
Adding $H^*$ shows that a derived membership overlap descriptor can retain a larger interaction component even when the score means are nearly additive.
Therefore, the factorial design serves not as a model-ranking device but as a diagnostic tool to determine whether an evaluation metric remains separable under independently varied prompt-side and decoding-side controls.

This distinction also explains why the membership specification is part of the method, rather than an incidental implementation detail.
This study does not claim that the sigmoid-S family is a uniquely correct representation of emotion intensity.
Instead, it fixes a smooth and reproducible Low/Medium/High specification, reports normalized entropy within that family, and checks the legacy linear family as a sensitivity baseline.
Under this interpretation, fuzzy entropy is a controlled descriptor of membership overlap in the scoring pipeline, rather than a direct measurement of human emotional uncertainty.

\section{Limitations and Extension}
The results were design-conditional.
They describe the selected six-model panel, three Japanese literary texts, four persona stances, four temperature levels, parser, response schema, and primary membership family.
They do not establish human validity, cross-lingual generality, or provider-independent causal effects.
The selected texts are meaningful literary emotion stimuli, but they are not random samples of Japanese literature.
Likewise, display model names should not be read as stable scientific objects, independent of provider updates.

The repeat depth was intentionally modest.
With $r=5$, the bootstrap intervals summarize repeat-level stability within the realized panel; they do not quantify the uncertainty over future model versions or broader text collections.
Fuzzy entropy analysis is also specification-dependent.
We address this by normalizing the entropy within the chosen membership family and retaining the legacy linear family as a sensitivity baseline, but no membership family should be interpreted as uniquely correct.
A journal extension should increase the repeat depth, broaden the model and text panels, add human-reference validation, and test robustness under additional membership and model-update conditions.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a fully crossed persona-temperature design for LLM-based fuzzy emotion scoring and applied it to a six-model Japanese literary emotion experiment.
The main result is that score variation is largely separable and persona-driven within the selected model-text-emotion subtables, whereas normalized fuzzy entropy retains a stronger persona-temperature interaction, particularly for anger and surprise.
This distinction would be obscured in a diagonal design that bundles the persona and temperature.
The practical lesson is that prompt-side interpretive controls and decoding-side stochastic controls should be independently varied when both are used in LLM-based affective scoring.
Under such a design, mean scores and membership-overlap entropy can summarize different separability profiles, even when they are computed from the same structured responses.
