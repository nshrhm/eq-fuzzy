\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly used as scalable judges of literary
emotion, but persona prompts and decoding temperature are often varied together.
This coupling makes it difficult to distinguish interpretive stance effects from
stochastic decoding effects. We introduce a fully crossed persona-temperature
design for Japanese literary emotion scoring and evaluate score, validity, and
normalized fuzzy entropy within the same balanced factorial structure. The main
run uses six contemporary models, three literary texts, four persona stances,
four temperature levels, and five repeats per cell, yielding 1440 responses and
5760 emotion-level scores. After retrying only four initially invalid rows, all
records passed the structured-output gate. Balanced decomposition summaries show
that score variation is dominated by persona effects, whereas normalized fuzzy
entropy carries larger persona-temperature interaction burdens, especially for
anger and surprise. The results show that independent crossing is needed not
only for cleaner score attribution, but also for detecting when ambiguity-related
metrics remain entangled across prompting and sampling controls.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLM-based emotion scoring offers a practical way to compare affective
interpretations across models and prompt settings. In prior work on emotional
individuality, persona prompting and decoding temperature were used to induce
diversity in model responses. However, when a specific persona is paired with a
specific temperature, the resulting score variation cannot be cleanly assigned
to either interpretive stance or decoding stochasticity. This is a design
limitation rather than only a statistical limitation: no downstream model can
separate two factors that were not independently varied.

This paper addresses that limitation with a fully crossed persona-temperature
design. We hold the text set and response schema fixed, express persona only as
an interpretive stance in the prompt, and pass temperature only as an API
configuration parameter. The central question is whether persona effects,
temperature effects, and their interaction can be described separately for
score-level emotion ratings and fuzzy entropy derived from those ratings.

Our contribution is methodological and empirical. First, we define a
conference-scale factorial protocol for LLM-based literary emotion scoring.
Second, we compute balanced effect components for persona, temperature, and
persona-temperature interaction under the same cell structure for score and
entropy. Third, we update the fuzzy entropy pipeline to use a smooth
sigmoid-composed membership family as the primary specification, while retaining
the prior piecewise-linear family as a sensitivity baseline. The intended claim
is not that one membership function is uniquely correct, but that the factorial
comparison is made under an explicit and reproducible membership specification.

\section{Related Work}
LLM emotion benchmarks have evaluated affective understanding, empathy, and
emotion recognition from several perspectives. These efforts establish that LLMs
can be compared on emotion-related tasks, but they do not by themselves resolve
how prompt persona and sampling parameters affect score formation. Our setting
is narrower: we focus on repeated literary emotion scoring under a controlled
factorial design.

Fuzzy-set theory provides a natural language for representing degrees of
membership rather than crisp class labels \cite{zadeh1965fuzzy}. Following the
Shannon-type entropy tradition \cite{shannon1948}, prior EQ-Fuzzy work used
Low, Medium, and High membership values over 0--100 emotion intensity scores to
summarize ambiguity in LLM scoring \cite{shirahama2026mdpi}. In this paper, we
keep the semantic breakpoints comparable to the prior implementation, but smooth
the transition shape with a sigmoid-composed S-function membership family. This
keeps the analysis comparable to the earlier EQ-Fuzzy setting while reducing
dependence on piecewise-linear breakpoints.

\section{Method}
Let $x_{m,t,p,\tau,r,e}$ denote a 0--100 score produced by model $m$ for text
$t$, persona $p$, temperature $\tau$, repeat $r$, and emotion $e$. The primary
factorial cell is $(m,t,p,\tau)$, with four emotion dimensions recorded for each
response. For each model-text-emotion unit, we summarize the $4 \times 4$
persona-temperature mean table with three balanced components:
\begin{equation}
SS_{\mathrm{persona}}, \quad SS_{\mathrm{temp}}, \quad
SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}}.
\end{equation}
We report the relative interaction burden
\begin{equation}
B =
\frac{SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}}}
{SS_{\mathrm{persona}} + SS_{\mathrm{temp}} +
SS_{\mathrm{persona}\times\mathrm{temp}}},
\end{equation}
and separability share $1-B$. Low interaction burden indicates that persona and
temperature are practically separable for the outcome under analysis. The
decomposition is descriptive: it quantifies balanced factorial variation in the
observed panel, not causal effects of API temperature across providers.

For fuzzy entropy, each score is mapped to Low, Medium, and High membership
values. The primary family, denoted `sigmoid\_s\_v1', preserves the prior
breakpoints while smoothing the transition shape. We compute raw fuzzy entropy
$H_{\mathrm{raw}} = -\sum_k \mu_k \log_2 \mu_k$ with $0\log 0=0$, and report
the normalized value $H^* = H_{\mathrm{raw}}/H_{\max}$, where $H_{\max}$ is
deterministically estimated for each membership family on a dense score grid.

\section{Experiments}
The main experiment used six contemporary LLMs, three Japanese literary texts,
four persona stances, four temperature levels ($0.1$, $0.4$, $0.7$, and $0.9$),
and five repeats per condition. This gives
$6 \times 3 \times 4 \times 4 \times 5 = 1440$ response trials. Each response
was required to return four emotion scores and corresponding reasons in the
fixed response schema. The model panel consisted of GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.5,
Gemini 2.5 Pro, Grok 4.20, DeepSeek V3.2, and Llama 4 Maverick. Temperature was
never mentioned in the prompt text; persona was expressed only as the
interpretive stance.

The initial main run contained four invalid rows. We repaired these by retrying
only the failed manifest rows and merging the successful retry records into a
separate repaired raw file, leaving the original raw output unchanged. The
repaired run reached complete structured-output validity, as shown in
Table~\ref{tab:scis-validity}.

\section{Results and Discussion}
Table~\ref{tab:scis-bootstrap-ci} summarizes the primary decomposition with
within-cell bootstrap intervals. The resampling keeps model, text, persona, and
temperature cells fixed and resamples the five repeats within each cell,
preserving the four emotion scores from the same response as a block. For mean
score, persona accounts for most of the decomposed variation across all four
emotions. The mean persona share is above 90\% for interest, sadness, and
surprise, and remains above 80\% for anger. Temperature shares are comparatively
small. Score-level interaction burdens range from 3.4\% for sadness to 13.2\%
for anger, so score variation is largely separable under the crossed design.
\input{../../artifacts/scis2026/main_bootstrap_v1/effect_summary_bootstrap_ci}

The entropy results show a different pattern. Although persona remains the
largest average component for $H^*$, interaction burdens are higher than for
score in every emotion. They reach 28.3\% for anger and 19.8\% for surprise,
compared with 13.2\% and 7.0\% for the corresponding score summaries. This
means that score means and entropy descriptors answer different questions:
scores identify the dominant interpretive stance effect, while entropy reveals
where the placement of scores relative to Low, Medium, and High remains jointly
sensitive to persona and temperature.

The entropy sensitivity check in Table~\ref{tab:scis-entropy-sensitivity}
compares the primary smooth membership family with the prior piecewise-linear
baseline. The cell-mean correlation is high, indicating that the updated
membership family does not create an unrelated entropy signal. At the same
time, the nonzero absolute differences confirm that membership-family choice
affects entropy magnitude. We therefore interpret $H^*$ comparatively under a
fixed family, and use the legacy family as a sensitivity baseline rather than as
a competing primary endpoint.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a fully crossed persona-temperature design for LLM-based fuzzy
emotion scoring and applied it to a six-model Japanese literary emotion
experiment. The main result is that score variation is largely separable and
persona dominated, whereas normalized fuzzy entropy retains stronger
persona-temperature interaction, particularly for anger and surprise. This
distinction would be obscured in a diagonal design that bundles persona and
temperature together. The study is limited to selected models, selected
Japanese literary texts, and a fixed fuzzy-membership specification; it should
not be read as a universal claim about emotional ambiguity. Future work should
increase repeat depth, add external validation, and test whether the same
separability pattern holds across broader text and model panels.
