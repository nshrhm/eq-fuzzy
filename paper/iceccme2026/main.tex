\begin{abstract}
Human-grounded validation remains a central gap in large language model (LLM) evaluation for affective text understanding. Existing benchmarks and multilingual profiling can reveal model-to-model or language-to-language variation, but such variation alone does not show which systems are closest to human judgments. This study proposes a compact Japanese-human-grounded benchmark for literary emotion evaluation using three Japanese literary works and four continuous affective dimensions: interest, surprise, sadness, and anger. Japanese Visual Analog Scale (VAS) ratings from student readers were used to define the reference profile, and six API-accessible LLMs were compared using a unified multiprovider experimental platform. The primary endpoint was alignment with the Japanese human reference under a neutral reader prompt. The English and Chinese conditions are treated as translated-condition robustness probes, not as co-equal human-grounded endpoints. Claude Sonnet 4.5 had the lowest observed mean absolute error to the Japanese human reference (20.03), with the top four models tightly clustered within 1.09 points, whereas Gemini 2.5 Pro showed the largest error (28.95). GPT-5.4 had the highest observed Pearson and Spearman agreement over the 12 story-by-emotion cells, whereas Qwen3.6 Plus and GPT-5.4 exhibited the smallest average translated-condition drift (3.35 and 3.35, respectively). The results indicate that primary Japanese human alignment and translated-condition robustness are not identical in this panel, making the protocol suitable for compact, analysis-level, reproducible model screening.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
LLM evaluation for affective text understanding increasingly includes benchmark suites, multilingual tests, and human--AI comparisons. These approaches are valuable, but they often leave a critical endpoint hierarchy under-specified: model-to-model or language-to-language variation can show that outputs differ, but it cannot show whether those outputs are close to human judgments. Smaller human-versus-AI comparisons, in turn, often focus on a single model or a single language condition \cite{shirahama2025vas,paech2024eqbench,huang2024emotionbench}. A compact benchmark is therefore useful only if it keeps the human reference explicit and does not treat multilingual stability as a substitute for human validity.

This paper addresses that gap by using Japanese Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) human ratings as the reference profile and treating the Japanese condition as the primary endpoint. English and Chinese were analyzed only as robustness conditions for translated-condition drift. Therefore, this contribution is not a generic language-effect study but a compact Japanese-human-grounded framework that separates primary Japanese human alignment from secondary-language robustness.

The present paper makes three concrete contributions. First, it provides a reproducible benchmark design that keeps the primary claim tightly anchored to Japanese human reference data. Second, it separates human alignment from translated-condition robustness so that secondary language behavior is not overinterpreted as a substitute for human validity. Third, it offers a two-axis screening view---Japanese calibration versus translated-condition drift---that is practical for compact engineering evaluations before larger multilingual or multi-persona studies are undertaken.

\section{Related Work}
The present study sits at the intersection of four lines of work. The first compares human and AI emotion judgments and motivates the use of continuous scales rather than coarse categorical labels. VAS instruments are a standard way to record subjective intensity on a continuous line \cite{aitken1969vas}, and our earlier VAS-versus-Likert study showed that a VAS-based design is practical for short story affect evaluation and provides a stronger foundation for fine-grained human--LLM comparison than a small discrete scale \cite{shirahama2025vas}.

The second line concerns benchmark design for LLM emotional intelligence. Benchmark suites such as EQ-Bench, EmotionBench, EmoBench, and EmotionQueen have helped standardize evaluation of emotional reasoning, empathy, and affective response quality \cite{paech2024eqbench,huang2024emotionbench,sabour2024emobench,chen2024emotionqueen}. However, these suites are not primarily designed to ask which current models are closest to a human reference profile on a literary reading task with continuous story-by-emotion scoring.

The third line concerns multilingual and robustness-oriented LLM evaluation. Cross-lingual benchmarks such as XNLI show the value of translated evaluation conditions while also making clear that language, translation, and task formulation are intertwined \cite{conneau2018xnli}. Language changes can alter lexical choice, narrative framing, and calibration, so multilingual comparison is necessary when a benchmark is meant to support deployment across translated conditions. Yet multilingual variation alone is not equivalent to human validity unless matched human references are also available.

The fourth line is our own prior platform-development and mathematical-characterization work. Those studies established that LLMs differ substantially in reliability, controllability, and emotional individuality across a broad model landscape \cite{openrouterplatform,shirahama2026mdpi}. The present paper intentionally narrows the design: one neutral-reader primary condition, one human-grounded language endpoint, and multilingual runs interpreted as robustness analyses rather than as direct substitutes for human validation.

\section{Data and Methods}
\textbf{Human reference data.} The benchmark uses respondent-level VAS data for three short Japanese literary texts and four affective dimensions: interest, surprise, sadness, and anger. The three works are Kyusaku Yumeno's \textit{The Pocket Watch} (T1; \textit{Kaichu dokei}), Kyusaku Yumeno's \textit{The Money and the Pistol} (T2; \textit{Okane to pisutoru}), and Kotaro Takamura's \textit{The Tattered Ostrich} (T3; \textit{Boroboro na dacho}). The Japanese source texts are public Aozora Bunko texts, with fixed author-prepared English and Chinese translations used for robustness probes \cite{aozora}. The task was administered in Japanese to student readers as a VAS survey, in which each respondent rated the three texts on the same four emotions after reading each work. Before participation, respondents were informed in Japanese that participation was voluntary, collected data would be anonymized and statistically analyzed, the results could be used for international conference presentations or academic papers, and data-use withdrawal was possible after submission by contacting the instructor. Ninety-one human responses were collected, and the retained story-by-emotion cells had 86--91 usable ratings. The public repository only stores sanitized numeric derivatives. The present analysis reuses only anonymized secondary numeric derivatives of the student VAS data; raw survey exports, respondent identifiers, and platform metadata were not redistributed.

\textbf{Texts and model panel.} The computational benchmark evaluates the same three works used in the human task and compares six API-accessible LLMs, one per provider family, where possible, rather than an exhaustive leaderboard. The exact OpenRouter slugs were \texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4.5}, \texttt{openai/gpt-5.4}, \texttt{x-ai/grok-4.20}, \texttt{qwen/qwen3.6-plus}, \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-v3.2}, and \texttt{google/gemini-2.5-pro.} Each model was queried in Japanese, English, and Chinese languages. The model panel was intentionally kept compact to match the engineering pilot scope of this study while preserving vendor diversity.

\textbf{Task definition.} The neutral-reader model run operationalized the same four affect dimensions on a 0--100 scale to approximate the human VAS task. One LLM experimental unit consisted of one model, language, text, neutral reader prompt, and repetition. The model received the full literary text, the neutral reader’s role, the four emotion definitions, and the scoring rule that 0 means the emotion is not felt at all, 50 means moderately felt, and 100 means extremely strongly felt. It returned one JSON object containing four integer scores and brief text-grounded explanations. A single neutral-reader prompt was used in all languages so that the benchmark would remain interpretable as a human alignment pilot rather than collapse back into persona-conditioned evaluation. Japanese human alignment was the primary endpoint because the reference VAS data were collected in Japanese, with Japanese source texts and Japanese task instructions used in the primary human--LLM alignment condition. English and Chinese are therefore not treated as co-equal human-grounded evaluations but as translated-condition robustness probes; in those runs, both the literary texts and the task instructions were language-matched using fixed author-prepared translations, so drift reflects the combined effect of translated text and prompt language rather than instruction language alone. Because the benchmark is intentionally compact, the analysis is interpreted descriptively for screening utility rather than formal significance testing. This design keeps persona-conditioned prompting outside the center of the present study, because persona and temperature were confounded in our earlier LLM-only design \cite{shirahama2026mdpi}.

\textbf{Execution provenance.} Model calls were collected through OpenRouter from 17--April 19, 2026 (JST). All manifest rows used the neutral-reader persona, API temperature 0.4, ten repetitions, JSON-schema structured outputs, and a local parser that accepted JSON or legacy Q-score text only when all four 0--100 emotion scores were recoverable. No seed or top-$p$ overrides were supplied. The maximum completion-token cap was 700 for the main run and up to 1600 for the provider-compatible repair runs. A targeted retry manifest covered 90 of the 540 rows and reused the same scoring task; no scores were imputed. All 540 manifest rows had valid parsed scores after targeted repair; exact API replay may vary because provider routing and model aliases can be changed.

\textbf{Aggregation.} Let $s \in \{1,2,3\}$ index texts and $d \in \{1,2,3,4\}$ index affect dimensions. The Japanese human reference profile is
\begin{equation}
H_{s,d}=\frac{1}{N_{s,d}}\sum_{i=1}^{N_{s,d}} y_{i,s,d},
\end{equation}
where $y_{i,s,d}$ is the VAS score from respondent $i$ and $N_{s,d}$ is the number of usable human ratings for text $s$ and affect dimension $d$ after sanitation. The aggregate human reference uses all usable ratings available for each story-by-emotion cell; the 86 complete cases were reported to characterize respondent-level completeness. For model $m$, language $\ell \in \{\mathrm{JA},\mathrm{EN},\mathrm{ZH}\}$, and repetition count $R=10$, the aggregated model profile is
\begin{equation}
M_{m,\ell,s,d}=\frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R} x_{m,\ell,s,d,r}.
\end{equation}
This yielded a 12-value story-by-emotion profile for each model-language condition.

\textbf{Metrics.} The primary endpoint was Japanese human alignment. For each model, the Japanese scores were aggregated to a story-by-emotion profile and compared with the Japanese human reference using the mean absolute error (MAE), Pearson correlation, and Spearman rank correlation.
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MAE}_{m,\mathrm{JA}}=\frac{1}{12}\sum_{s,d}\left|M_{m,\mathrm{JA},s,d}-H_{s,d}\right|.
\end{equation}
English and Chinese were not treated as additional human-grounded end points. Instead, translated-condition drift was defined as the mean absolute difference between each model's English or Chinese story-by-emotion score profile and the same model's Japanese profile.
\begin{equation}
D_{m,\ell}=\frac{1}{12}\sum_{s,d}\left|M_{m,\ell,s,d}-M_{m,\mathrm{JA},s,d}\right|,~~ \ell \in \{\mathrm{EN},\mathrm{ZH}\}.
\end{equation}
We also report the average drift
\begin{equation}
\bar{D}_m=\frac{D_{m,\mathrm{EN}}+D_{m,\mathrm{ZH}}}{2}.
\end{equation}
A lower MAE and drift are better. Because the benchmark is intentionally compact, this study emphasizes descriptive comparison and screening utility rather than formal significance testing.

\textbf{Protocol and reproducibility.} The protocol comprised 91 human responses, with 86 complete cases and 86--91 usable ratings per story-by-emotion cell after sanitation across three texts and four affect dimensions. The mean human within-cell standard deviation was 22.83 VAS points across 12 retained cells. The model side covered 6 models $\times$ 3 languages $\times$ 3 texts $\times$ 10 repetitions under one neutral-reader prompt, yielding 540 neutral-reader model runs, and 2160 numeric scores. The public repository supports analysis-level reproducibility through sanitized numeric derivatives, manifests, prompts, schemas, and scoring scripts; raw survey exports, model-free text justifications, and account-level API metadata are not redistributed.

\section{Results}
Table~\ref{tab:design} summarizes the benchmark design, Figure~\ref{fig:ja-ranking} shows the Japanese human-alignment ranking, Figure~\ref{fig:drift} shows translated-condition drift relative to Japanese, Table~\ref{tab:primary} reports the core summary statistics, and Figure~\ref{fig:tradeoff} visualizes the two-axis screening view.

\textbf{Japanese human alignment.} In the primary Japanese condition, Claude Sonnet 4.5 had the lowest observed mean absolute error (MAE) to the human reference (20.026), followed by DeepSeek V3.2 (20.100), Qwen3.6 Plus (20.744), GPT-5.4 (21.113), and Grok 4.20 (21.410), whereas Gemini 2.5 Pro showed the largest error (28.952). The spread between the best and worst models was therefore 8.926 points. Simultaneously, the top four models formed a near-tie cluster within only 1.087 MAE points, indicating that model screening would be sensitive to relatively small observed calibration differences within the stronger half of the panel.

GPT-5.4 yielded both the highest observed Pearson correlation with the Japanese human profile ($r = 0.475$) and the highest observed Spearman rank correlation ($\rho = 0.441$), which was computed descriptively over the 12 story-by-emotion cells. This split is important because absolute calibration and rank-order agreement should be distinguished rather than collapsed into a single score. Claude Sonnet 4.5 had the lowest observed absolute intensity error, whereas GPT-5.4 best preserved relative ordering in this compact panel.

\textbf{Translated-condition robustness.} For translated-condition robustness, Qwen3.6 Plus exhibited the smallest average drift relative to Japanese ($\bar{D}=3.346$), followed closely by GPT-5.4 ($\bar{D}=3.354$), and Gemini 2.5 Pro ($\bar{D}=4.729$). By contrast, DeepSeek V3.2 showed the largest average drift ($\bar{D}=6.396$), with the largest single-language drift observed in its English condition (7.875). Across all models and secondary languages, drift ranged from 2.983 to 7.875 points, reflecting the combined translated text and language-matched prompt condition rather than a pure language effect.

That difference is descriptive rather than inferential, but it still shows why robustness should not be assumed to be symmetric across translated conditions.

This separation is important for deployment-oriented evaluation because a model may align relatively well with human intensity levels in the primary language while showing different stability under translated conditions, or vice versa.

\section{Discussion}
Three points follow from these results. First, translated-condition variation is not the same as human validity. A model can appear plausible under translated prompting yet still diverge substantially from the Japanese human reference when calibration is evaluated directly. This is the main reason we treat Japanese human alignment as the primary endpoint and EN/ZH behavior as robustness analysis rather than as co-equal human-grounded evaluation.

Second, a compact benchmark can still produce operationally useful screening information if its rankings are interpreted descriptively. In the primary Japanese condition, Claude Sonnet 4.5 had the lowest observed MAE to the human reference (20.026), but the top four models were separated by only 1.087 MAE points each. The mean human within-cell SD of 22.83 VAS points indicates substantial reader-level variability; therefore, the reported MAE values should be interpreted as agreement with an aggregate Japanese reader profile rather than as replacement-level agreement with individual readers. GPT-5.4 yielded the highest Japanese Pearson and Spearman correlations (0.475 and 0.441, respectively) over 12 cells, indicating that absolute calibration and rank-order agreement should be distinguished rather than collapsed into a single score. For translated-condition robustness, Qwen3.6 Plus and GPT-5.4 showed the smallest average EN/ZH drift (3.346 and 3.354, respectively), whereas DeepSeek V3.2 showed the largest average drift (6.396). Therefore, no single model dominates both human alignment and translation condition robustness.

Operationally, the panel should be read as a trade-off map, rather than a leaderboard. Claude Sonnet 4.5 is the lowest-MAE Japanese model in this run, DeepSeek V3.2 is close on Japanese MAE but has the largest average drift, Qwen3.6 Plus and GPT-5.4 are the most stable under EN/ZH translated conditions, and Gemini 2.5 Pro shows that moderate translated-condition stability does not imply strong Japanese human alignment. That makes the protocol useful for early-stage model narrowing before a larger validation campaign is launched.

Third, the paper clarifies how this ICECCME contribution differs from our earlier LLM-only framework paper. The previous study was designed to characterize emotional individuality under persona-assigned temperatures across a broad 36-model landscape \cite{shirahama2026mdpi}. The present paper instead fixes a neutral-reader primary condition and uses human alignment as the primary endpoint. This shift makes the paper more appropriate for a compact engineering conference contribution focused on benchmark design and cross-language robustness rather than on multidimensional individuality analysis.

This neutral-reader constraint is methodological, not merely stylistic. Once role-playing or creativity prompts are introduced, it becomes harder to know whether score changes reflect better human alignment, a different interpretive stance, or prompt-induced variance. For a human-grounded pilot benchmark, anchoring the main endpoint to one neutral condition makes the comparison easier to interpret and reproduce across providers. Richer persona-conditioned evaluation remains worthwhile, but it should be layered onto a stable human-reference baseline rather than replacing it.

The benchmark should be understood as a stage-1 filter rather than as a deployment certificate. Its main limitations are that it uses only three texts, one Japanese student-reader reference condition, and one neutral-reader prompt condition with ten repetitions per model--language--text condition. English and Chinese are robustness conditions rather than human-grounded endpoints, and the measured drift reflects both translated literary texts and language-matched task instructions rather than instruction language alone. The English and Chinese translations were fixed, author-prepared translations and were not independently validated against separate human affect references. The analysis is descriptive for screening utility rather than formal significance testing, and exact API replay is subject to provider routing and model version changes, even when manifests and scripts are preserved. Provider-compatibility adjustments were also required at the transport/schema layer during data collection, although the task definition, source texts, and scoring dimensions were kept fixed. The next steps are to layer new evidence in a controlled manner: more texts, larger and more diverse human reference samples, and matched human references for English and Chinese.

\section{Conclusion}
This study presents a compact Japanese-human-grounded benchmark for literary emotion evaluation in contemporary large language models. Using a Japanese VAS reference from student readers, we compared six API-accessible models under a neutral-reader prompt and found that Claude Sonnet 4.5 had the lowest observed absolute error to the Japanese human profile, GPT-5.4 had the highest observed Pearson and Spearman agreement over 12 cells, and Qwen3.6 Plus had the smallest average translated condition drift. More broadly, the results indicate that primary Japanese human alignment and translated-condition robustness are not identical in this study. We therefore recommend reporting them independently in future LLM emotion-evaluation studies and using compact human-grounded benchmarks as an initial screening step before larger multilingual or multi-persona evaluations.
