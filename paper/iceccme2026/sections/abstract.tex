Human-grounded validation remains a major gap in large language model (LLM) evaluation for affective text understanding. Prior work has shown that models exhibit distinctive emotion-scoring patterns on literary texts, but model-to-model differences alone do not reveal which systems are actually closest to human judgments. This paper proposes a compact human-grounded multilingual benchmark for literary emotion evaluation using three Japanese literary works and four continuous affect dimensions: interest, surprise, sadness, and anger. We use Japanese Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) ratings from student readers as the reference profile and compare six contemporary LLMs accessed through a unified multi-provider experimental platform. The primary analysis evaluates alignment with the Japanese human profile under a neutral-reader prompt in Japanese, while English and Chinese conditions are treated as robustness tests for cross-language drift. Claude Sonnet 4.5 achieved the lowest mean absolute error (20.026) to the Japanese human reference, whereas Gemini 2.5 Pro showed the largest error (28.952). Qwen3.6 Plus and GPT-5.4 exhibited the smallest average cross-language drift (3.346 and 3.354, respectively), while DeepSeek V3.2 showed the largest (6.396). The benchmark is compact enough for reproducible model screening while preserving a clear separation between primary human alignment and multilingual robustness.
